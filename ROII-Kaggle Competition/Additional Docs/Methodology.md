Methodology for Subsurface Modeling: Correlating Gamma Ray Signatures and Geological Dip in Horizontal Wellbores
1. Strategic Context of Gamma Ray and TVT Integration
In the precision-driven environment of horizontal well navigation, the correlation of Gamma Ray (GR) signatures with True Vertical Thickness (TVT) is a strategic necessity. This relationship serves as the technical backbone for reservoir characterization, allowing geosteering teams to ascertain the wellbore's precise position within a targeted stratigraphic interval. By establishing a dynamic link between petrophysical radioactivity and vertical depth profiles, operators can mitigate the risks of "exiting the window" and optimize the placement of the lateral within the highest-quality rock.The primary objective of this methodology is the accurate calculation of TVT values beyond the Prediction Start (PS) point. This is achieved by synthesizing multi-dimensional horizontal well data—including Measured Depth (MD), spatial XYZ coordinates, and real-time GR logs—with established vertical typewell data consisting of known TVT, GR signatures, and geological layer markers. This data fusion transforms historical drilling records into a predictive engine for the unexplored lateral.
2. Comparative Data Architecture: Horizontal vs. Typewell Profiles
A robust subsurface model depends on the harmonization of disparate data formats from horizontal and vertical sources. An essential consideration in this architecture is the variance in data resolution. Gamma Ray data acquired from the horizontal well often exhibits a significantly higher resolution and greater sensitivity to lithological changes than standard typewell GR logs. However, horizontal logs frequently contain "NaN" (Not a Number) values at specific intervals which require careful handling. The superior granularity of the horizontal "fingerprint" allows for highly accurate correlation, provided the geosteering consultant accounts for these resolution gaps when aligning the local signature with the broader typewell trends.
3. Dynamics of Gamma Ray Behavior and TVT Fluctuations
The diagnostic value of Gamma Ray signatures lies in their ability to reflect geological trends as the wellbore traverses various strata. By projecting the horizontal GR log onto a TVT track, we identify three primary markers that dictate the perceived movement of the formation relative to the well path:
Increasing TVT:  When the horizontal GR signature matches the typewell at progressively deeper vertical values, it indicates a falling geological profile (formation is dipping away/deeper) relative to the well path.
Decreasing TVT:  When matches occur at shallower vertical values, it indicates a rising geological profile (formation is moving toward the surface) relative to the well path.
Constant TVT:  A "Flat" geological state exists when the GR signature remains stable and parallel to the typewell, indicating the wellbore is tracking perfectly within a specific bedding plane.From a consultant’s perspective, a constant GR signature should be viewed with technical caution. While it indicates a flat profile, it also represents a significant "blind spot" for geosteering algorithms. Without crossing lithological boundaries, the model loses vertical control, providing zero feedback on where the well sits within the target formation. In thin-bedded reservoirs like the EGFDU or EGFDL, this lack of signature variation increases the risk of an unforced exit from the target zone.
4. Geometric Influences: Azimuthal Impacts on Geological Dip
The orientation of the wellbore relative to the regional stress field and tectonic tilt—defined by the drilling azimuth—is a primary driver of perceived geological dip. A formation that appears flat at one heading may show significant dip when the azimuth is shifted.The relationship between drilling azimuth and perceived dip is evidenced by specific operational data points:
Azimuth -249.22°:  Observations at this heading typically show the geology in a dipping state, requiring active TVT adjustments to remain in-zone.
Azimuth 41.20°:  At this heading, geological formations such as the EGFDU and EGFDL often appear almost flat, simplifying wellbore placement.
Azimuth -47.00°:  This orientation reveals a consistent dip in a predictable direction.By synthesizing these azimuthal trends, a geosteering expert can anticipate whether formations will appear to rise or fall based on the planned trajectory, allowing for proactive rather than reactive steering.
5. Geosteering Strategy: Navigating the Prediction Start (PS) Threshold
The Prediction Start (PS) point is the critical temporal and spatial boundary where measured historical data ends and predictive modeling begins. It acts as the anchor for the entire lateral model.The strategy involves using the  TVT_input  data—the confirmed geological depths—recorded  before  the PS point (e.g., at an MD of 13,772 ft or 12,394 ft). To project the geology beyond this threshold, the methodology utilizes the high-resolution horizontal GR data collected prior to the PS. By correlating this "known" horizontal history against the deeper TVT data of the typewell, the PS point serves as the pivot where the model transitions from tracking the past to forecasting the future.
6. The Offset Well Paradigm: Leveraging Neighboring Trends
The spatial correlation of geological features suggests that trends observed in neighboring wells are highly likely to persist in the current wellbore. Leveraging regional well density through an "Offset Well" strategy significantly increases predictive reliability.In this paradigm, geosteering software utilizes color-coded log tracks to differentiate data sources: the  Green GR  (Horizontal well before the PS), the  Red GR  (Horizontal well after the PS), and the  Black GR  (the Vertical Typewell). Crucially, the Green GR recorded in the local lateral often correlates more accurately with the Red GR than the Black GR does. This highlights the necessity of relying on local wellbore history and trends from nearby offset wells to guide the model, as the vertical typewell may lack the local nuances of the horizontal section's petrophysical character.
7. Performance Benchmarking: Quantifying Prediction Quality
To validate the subsurface model, objective metrics must be applied to measure the variance between predicted and actual geological surfaces.The primary metric used is  dTVT , representing the delta between the manual (actual) TVT and the predicted TVT for every one-foot (1 ft) step beyond the Prediction Start point:$$dTVT = manualTVT - predictedTVT$$The ultimate KPI for measuring prediction quality across the entire lateral is the  Root Mean Square Error (RMSE)  of all dTVT values. By aggregating these deviations, the subsurface team can quantify the model's accuracy. The strategic goal of this methodology is the continuous minimization of RMSE to ensure maximum reservoir contact and long-term drilling efficiency.
